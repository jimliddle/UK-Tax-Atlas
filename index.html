1. How to use this Atlas

This is designed as a navigable atlas rather than a single crowded graphic. Choose a category, search a term, or switch tabs to see hidden, local, wealth or future tax-base layers.

Use of AI

Multiple local and foundational AI models where used when constructing this tax atlas and also for checking and vetting it. Part of the integrity checks included mapping back to the specific UK government website that lays out the legal basis for any tax or policy.

Policy maturity legend

🟢 Current charge = currently paid or legally active. 🟡 Government policy / implementation underway = announced, legislated or scheduled but not necessarily fully charged yet. 🟠 Strong policy direction = active consultation or clear fiscal trajectory. 🔴 Speculative = think-tank, commentator or long-range scenario.

Reading "payer" vs "bearer": statutory vs economic incidence

This atlas separates statutory incidence (who is legally liable to pay) from economic incidence (who ultimately bears the cost). Items such as employer National Insurance, business rates and corporation tax are deliberately listed as household burdens because their economic incidence falls on workers, consumers or savers. This is an analytical modelling choice, not an HMRC classification. The Corporate taxes (diffuse incidence) group is the clearest example: real taxes a household helps pay through prices, wages or returns, but never sees on a bill or payslip.

Why a Tax Atlas?

It has become increasingly difficult to map out the ways that UK citizens are taxed as many taxes are not that visible. In the past mapping out something of this nature would take a room full of consultants but with the advent of deep research AI and the use of critical thinking, it is now easier to attempt to get a handle on the tax taxonomy.

3. Local government revenue extraction

This tab captures the important shift from a single council-tax bill to a layered local burden: premiums, precepts, carved-out services, permits, licensing and local visitor/tourism instruments.

Service unbundling

A local authority may keep Council Tax politically constrained while charging separately for garden waste, bulky waste, extra bins, permits or licensing. Legally these are usually fees or charges, but for households they can feel like local tax increases.

6. Wealth, assets and inheritance

This separates current law from political labels and proposals. The “farmer tax” is shown as an IHT relief-reform issue, not a new standalone tax. A general wealth tax on homes over £2m is shown as proposed/speculative unless enacted.

7. AI, Sovereign AI and the future tax base

Scenario analysis, not prediction: if AI changes the wage share, ownership of AI assets and where profits accrue may become central to tax capacity.

Current fiscal model

Worker → salary → PAYE/NIC → consumption → VAT/excise.

The state has a clean collection point when humans are paid through payroll and spend through VAT-registered businesses.

AI-agent production model

Agent → output → owner captures rent.

The tax question shifts from “who worked?” to “who owns the model, compute, data, platform and customer relationship?”
